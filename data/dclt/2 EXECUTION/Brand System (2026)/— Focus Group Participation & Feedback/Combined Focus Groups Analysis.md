# Combined Focus Groups Analysis

## Door County Land Trust Rebrand Research: Legacy Conservationists & Active Participants

**Focus Group 1 Date:** June 20, 2024

**Focus Group 2 Date:** July 11, 2025

**Combined Duration:** 180 minutes total

**Purpose:** Comprehensive feedback on Door County Land Trust's visual rebranding efforts and community engagement strategies across key stakeholder segments

**Facilitator:** Andrew Goldsworthy

**Brand Designer:** Don Krumpos

**Land Trust Staff:** Lucia, Cinnamon

---

## Participant Demographics & Comparative Analysis

### Focus Group 1: Active Participants (Younger, New-to-County)

- **Rex:** 5th generation local, business owner, hunter inclusion advocate
- **Miriam:** Former Chicago resident, conservation volunteer, restoration work
- **Mike:** Non-resident with county ties, seeks volunteer opportunities
- **Tawnie:** Lifelong resident, nature photographer, working professional

### Focus Group 2: Legacy Conservationists (Older, Established)

- **Carolyn:** 50+ years in county, retired real estate professional, conservation easement holder
- **Bruce Pikas:** Conservation easement holder, primarily volunteers with The Ridges
- **Carol & Bruce Olson:** Former intensive volunteers (25+ years), stepped back due to organizational changes
- **Donna Hermann:** Multi-generational family connection, parents were early volunteers

### **Key Demographic Contrasts:**

- **Age Range:** Group 1 (30s-50s) vs Group 2 (60s-80s)
- **County Connection:** Group 1 (newer arrivals, seekers) vs Group 2 (multi-generational, established)
- **Engagement Style:** Group 1 (active seekers, digital-forward) vs Group 2 (relationship-focused, print-oriented)
- **Organizational Relationship:** Group 1 (discovering, building) vs Group 2 (established, transitioning)

---

## Cross-Group Topic Analysis

### 1. Door County Challenges - Generational Perspectives

### **Development Pressure - Dual Viewpoints:**

**Group 1 (Current Impact):**

- **Rex's observation:** *"There used to be a lot of land that was undeveloped... you watch those get cut off and sold and sold again"*
- **Housing crisis connection:** Less housing for full-time residents, concentrated developments
- **Present-focused:** Current visible changes and immediate impacts

**Group 2 (Historical Context):**

- **Carolyn's 50-year perspective:** *"The development is a real threat to the county... I've seen this over the last 50 years"*
- **Real estate insight:** Farmers being taken advantage of, not understanding current land values
- **Pattern recognition:** *"I get usually about 3 or 4 letters a week offering to... sell it"*

### **Water Quality - Technical vs. Lived Experience:**

**Group 1 (Scientific Understanding):**

- **Miriam's technical analysis:** *"You have the karst topography that makes it very quick to absorb all of the phosphates and nitrates from farming"*
- **Recent examples:** Pebble Beach salmonella issues
- **Systemic solutions:** *"It's gonna require an alignment for everybody who's up here"*

**Group 2 (Long-term Observation):**

- Less technical discussion but deeper concern about landscape character changes
- Focus on preserving existing natural features (meadows, mixed forests)
- Historical awareness of what's been lost over decades

### 2. Land Trust Role Definition - Consistent Priorities

### **Land Protection Primacy - Universal Agreement:**

**Group 1 Consensus:**

- **Miriam's framework:** *"Land Trust has historically been let's just get as many acres protected as humanly possible. And I guess I'm inclined to kind of agree with that"*
- **Specialization value:** *"Land Trust has a lane, and it's in it"*

**Group 2 Consensus:**

- **Unanimous agreement** across all participants
- **Bruce's statement:** *"Protection should be number one... the more land that can be protected the better off this county will be"*
- **Donna's validation:** *"I agree, because the other groups are doing so much more of the other pieces"*

### **Education Role - Demographic Differences:**

**Group 1 (Collaborative Approach):**

- Education through partnerships with other organizations
- Focus on experiential learning and volunteer participation
- Cross-pollination between conservation groups

**Group 2 (Direct Instruction):**

- **Carol's vision:** *"Offering classes... letting other people know, hopefully younger people, how important it is to protect this land"*
- Adult-focused education about conservation importance
- Specific content: meadow management, tree planting, land stewardship

### 3. Visual Brand Analysis - Generational Aesthetic Preferences

### **Mood Board Responses - Clear Patterns:**

**Group 1 Preferences:**

- **Mood Board B overwhelming favorite:** "Approachable," "choose your own adventure," bright and inviting
- **Rex:** *"More approachable... I like the orange, the brightness... more prettier"*
- **Design appreciation:** Trail maps, interactive elements, modern aesthetic

**Group 2 Preferences:**

- **Mood Board C strong preference:** Clean, uncluttered, professional
- **Carolyn:** *"I think I like C. It just seemed a little cleaner, a little less confusing"*
- **Bruce:** *"I probably like C the best too. It's not as cluttered"*

**Cross-Group Rejection:**

- **Both groups rejected Mood Board A** for being too dark, cluttered, or heavy
- **Both criticized Mood Board B colors** (Group 1 liked design but wanted darker colors; Group 2 found colors too light)

### **Font and Typography - Surprising Consensus:**

- **Both groups appreciated Mood Board B typography** once separated from color discussion
- Modern, clean fonts preferred across age demographics
- Readability and accessibility valued universally

### 4. Door County Representation - Unified Vision

### **Essential Visual Elements - Cross-Group Agreement:**

- **Water and bluffs:** Universal requirement across both groups
- **Mixed forests:** Both emphasized need for hardwood/pine combination, not just pine
- **Authentic landscapes:** No mountains, genuine Door County scenes
- **Human presence:** Trails, families, people using the land

### **Group-Specific Additions:**

**Group 1:**

- Hunter inclusion and outdoor recreation activities
- Diverse user groups and accessibility
- Modern, inclusive representation

**Group 2:**

- **Meadow representation:** Carolyn's passionate advocacy for prairie/meadow inclusion
- Historical landscape elements
- Traditional conservation imagery

### 5. Current Brand Assessment - Consistent Criticism

### **Logo Problems - Universal Agreement:**

**Group 1:**

- **Miriam:** *"I don't like it... it feels too formal and too sanitized"*
- **Rex:** *"It just looks outdated"*

**Group 2:**

- **Carol:** "Boring," "doesn't do anything for me"
- **Carolyn:** *"I do have a problem with whatever is going on with those trees that look like teepees"*

### **Positive Elements - Shared Recognition:**

- Archway/"window" concept appreciated by both groups
- Bluffs and water elements valued
- Bird inclusion noted positively

### 6. Content Strategy - Platform and Format Preferences

### **Communication Channels - Clear Generational Divide:**

**Group 1 (Multi-Channel Approach):**

- **Rex's video strategy:** *"Why not film it and post the whole thing on YouTube"*
- **Social media engagement:** Instagram, Facebook for discovery and engagement
- **Website usage:** Regular checking for volunteer opportunities and updates
- **Email as foundation:** But expects multi-platform content distribution

**Group 2 (Print-Centric):**

- **Bruce's preference:** *"I prefer mail... it's nice to be able to hold on to it"*
- **Physical materials:** Want tangible items to keep, reference, and share
- **Calendar enthusiasm:** Strong support for continued phenology calendar
- **Limited digital engagement:** Acknowledge importance for younger demographics but personally avoid

### **Content Priorities - Unified Interests:**

**Both Groups Value:**

1. **New property acquisitions** with detailed stories and visuals
2. **Conservation stories and history** - how properties came to protection
3. **Property characteristics and uniqueness**
4. **Landowner testimonials and motivations**

**Group-Specific Additions:Group 1:**

- Behind-the-scenes content, financial transparency
- User-generated content campaigns
- Volunteer experience stories

**Group 2:**

- Property history and previous ownership
- Educational content about land management
- Progress statistics and protection milestones

### 7. Volunteer Engagement - Different Needs, Shared Passion

### **Scheduling and Accessibility:**

**Group 1 (Working Professional Constraints):**

- **Tawnie's critique:** *"A lot of the organizations in Door County have a problem... They like to do things on a Tuesday at 9 in the morning. I work"*
- **Solution requests:** Evening and weekend opportunities
- **Target times:** 5-7 PM weekdays, Saturday mornings

**Group 2 (Flexibility with Relationship Focus):**

- **Large group events:** Donna's Ice Age Trail Alliance model inspiration
- **Personal connection emphasis:** Value for community building over efficiency
- **Rotation system:** Include more volunteers even if less efficient

### **Engagement Quality - Universal Desire:**

**Both Groups Want:**

- Meaningful, impactful volunteer work
- Connection to mission and other volunteers
- Recognition and feedback on contributions
- Clear communication about opportunities

### 8. Organizational Relationship - Different Expectations

### **Group 1 (Building Relationships):**

- Seeking to deepen engagement with growing organization
- Appreciative of professional development and capabilities
- Want more content and communication, not less
- **Rex's business perspective:** Opportunities for event participation and community presence

### **Group 2 (Relationship Transition):**

- **Nostalgia for "personal era":** Carol's emphasis on lost "warm and cuddly" feeling
- **Professional evolution challenges:** Feel displaced by staff growth and efficiency
- **Continued commitment:** Still donate and support mission despite relationship changes
- **Reconnection desire:** Want to feel valued and included again

## Strategic Implications - Cross-Group Synthesis

### **1. üéØ Brand Direction - Unified Framework**

### **Hybrid Visual Strategy:**

**"Modern Heritage with Authentic Community Voice"**

**Design System Recommendations:**

- **Primary aesthetic:** Clean, uncluttered design (Group 2 preference) with warm, approachable elements (Group 1 preference)
- **Color palette:** Darker, nature-inspired tones that satisfy both groups' Door County authenticity needs
- **Typography:** Modern, accessible fonts appreciated across age demographics
- **Imagery approach:** Authentic Door County landscapes with diverse human presence

**Multi-Tier Application:**

- **Public/Outreach:** Group 1-inspired approachable design for recruitment and engagement
- **Institutional/Legacy:** Group 2-inspired clean professionalism for donors and established supporters
- **Educational/Technical:** Systematic design for wayfinding, reports, and operational materials

### **2. üì¢ Communication Strategy - Multi-Channel Integration**

### **Platform Strategy by Demographic:**

**Digital-Forward (Group 1):**

- **Video content:** Long-form YouTube with social media clips
- **User-generated campaigns:** Incentivized community content creation
- **Email with multimedia:** Rich content with links to digital experiences
- **Social media discovery:** Instagram, Facebook for event promotion and engagement

**Print-Primary (Group 2):**

- **Enhanced newsletter:** Shorter, more visual, story-focused
- **Continued calendar:** High-value annual publication
- **Direct mail:** Important announcements and donor communications
- **Physical events:** Primary relationship-building channel

**Cross-Platform Content:**

- **Property acquisition stories:** Detailed in print, summarized digitally
- **Landowner testimonials:** Video for digital, transcribed for print
- **Educational content:** Deep-dive articles in print, quick tips digitally
- **Event promotion:** Multi-channel with appropriate timing for each audience

### **3. ü§ù Volunteer Program - Inclusive Engagement Model**

### **Scheduling Diversification:**

- **Evening opportunities:** 5-7 PM weekdays for working professionals
- **Weekend options:** Saturday morning programs
- **Large group events:** Quarterly major projects bringing community together
- **Flexible participation:** Multiple engagement levels and time commitments

### **Program Structure:**

- **Rotation system:** Ensure broader community inclusion (Group 2 request)
- **Skill-based matching:** Leverage professional expertise (Group 1 strength)
- **Mentorship opportunities:** Experienced volunteers guiding newcomers
- **Recognition programs:** Celebrate contributions across engagement levels

### **Active Participant Cultivation (Group 1):**

- **Deepening engagement:** Multiple pathways for increased involvement

- **Professional networking:** Business member events and corporate engagement

## Conclusion: Building Bridges Across Generations

This combined analysis reveals both significant generational differences and surprising areas of consensus among Door County Land Trust stakeholders. The path forward requires honoring diverse communication preferences and engagement styles while maintaining unified commitment to land protection.

**Key Insight:** Both groups deeply value authentic Door County representation and meaningful connection to conservation work, but through different channels and relationship styles.

**Primary Opportunity:** The organization's 30th anniversary provides perfect timing to celebrate legacy relationships while establishing contemporary engagement approaches that serve both established supporters and emerging community members.

**Success Foundation:** A flexible brand system that speaks authentically to Door County's character while accommodating diverse communication preferences and engagement styles across generational lines.

**Implementation Priority:** Begin with relationship rebuilding and visual identity refinement, then layer in expanded programming and communication enhancements that serve both demographic groups effectively.
