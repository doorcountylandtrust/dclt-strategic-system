<p style="text-align: center;"><span style="font-size: 18pt;"><strong>Native Plant and Tree Give-Away Aims
</strong></span><span style="font-size: 18pt;"><strong>to Improve Wildlife Habitat Throughout Door County</strong></span></p>
&nbsp;
<p data-start="151" data-end="445">The <strong data-start="155" data-end="168">Big Plant</strong>, coordinated by the <strong data-start="189" data-end="232">Climate Change Coalition of Door County</strong>, returns on <strong data-start="245" data-end="271">Saturday, May 17, 2025</strong>, 10 a.m. until the plants run out, with the Door County Land Trust’s native tree and plant giveaway—an opportunity to bring more pollinator-friendly perennials and native trees into our landscapes. The plant giveaway takes place in the Epicenter parking lot, outside the DCLT office, located at 217 N 4th Ave, Sturgeon Bay.</p>
<p data-start="447" data-end="771">In 2023, more than <strong data-start="466" data-end="482">15,000 trees</strong> were planted through The Big Plant thanks to the efforts of participating organizations. To ensure <strong data-start="586" data-end="598">everyone</strong> can take part, Door County Land Trust includes trees as well as <strong data-start="654" data-end="674">perennial plants</strong> in its giveaway, making it accessible to those with <strong data-start="727" data-end="768">small yards, balconies, or apartments</strong>.</p>

<blockquote>
<p data-start="775" data-end="1140">“Whether you have a planter on a porch, a small backyard, or several acres, you can plant something that helps native insects, birds, and wildlife,” says <strong data-start="929" data-end="949">Cinnamon Rossman</strong>, Door County Land Trust’s <strong data-start="976" data-end="1009">Director of Charitable Giving</strong>. “As these trees grow and flowers bloom, they’ll be a reminder of the things we can do to preserve and improve our environment.”</p>
</blockquote>
The 2025 giveaway features a variety of <strong data-start="1186" data-end="1202">native trees</strong>:
<ul>
 	<li><strong data-start="1209" data-end="1223">Balsam Fir</strong></li>
 	<li><strong data-start="1247" data-end="1261">White Pine</strong></li>
 	<li><strong data-start="1267" data-end="1283">White Spruce</strong></li>
</ul>
And <strong>pollinator-friendly perennials</strong>:
<ul>
 	<li><strong data-start="1433" data-end="1487">New England Aster</strong> (<em data-start="1454" data-end="1484">Symphyotrichum novae-angliae</em>)</li>
 	<li><strong data-start="1493" data-end="1529">Golden Alexander </strong>(<em data-start="1513" data-end="1526">Zizia aurea</em>)</li>
 	<li><strong data-start="1332" data-end="1379">Common Ironweed </strong>(<span data-teams="true">Vernonia fasciculata</span>)</li>
 	<li><span data-teams="true"><strong>Marsh Blazing Star</strong> (<i>Liatris spicata</i>)</span></li>
</ul>
<a href="https://www.doorcountylandtrust.org/wp-content/uploads/2025/05/2025-Big-Plant-Cards-1.pdf">Details about each of these can be found in this handy PDF.</a>

Each of these species is <strong data-start="1558" data-end="1582">hardy in Door County</strong> and supports <strong data-start="1596" data-end="1634">insects, birds, and other wildlife</strong>.
