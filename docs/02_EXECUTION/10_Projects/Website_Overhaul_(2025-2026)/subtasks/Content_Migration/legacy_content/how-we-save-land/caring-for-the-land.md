## Restoring and Maintaining Ecological Balance

### Restoring Forest Habitat

Before the first settlers arrived, Door County was forested from shore to shore with cedar, hemlock, fir, spruce, and other trees. Over time, forests were logged, burned, and cleared for agriculture â€” disrupting the ecological balance.

Now, steep cliffs and low-lying wetlands offer a window to the past. Native trees filter water, shade out invasive species, and provide essential habitat.

**Reforestation projects** help by:
- Replanting native trees
- Creating wildlife corridors
- Buffering run-off and erosion

_[Read more about recent tree-planting projects.](https://doorcountylandtrust.org/native-species-must-be-reintroduced-to-the-system/0)_

### Monitoring and Removing Invasive Species

Not all green is good. Invasive plants disrupt native habitats, outcompete rare species, and harm wildlife.

**Examples:**
- Honeysuckle berries sicken birds
- Buckthorn acidifies wetlands, harming amphibians
- Some invasives emit toxins to kill other plants

The Land Trust:
- Monitors spread
- Plans eradication
- Reintroduces native species

Restoration is a long-term process.  
Invasive species eradication is only the beginning.
